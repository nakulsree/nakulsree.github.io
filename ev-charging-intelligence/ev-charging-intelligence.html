01 / Overview

Executive Summary

California's push toward electrified transportation puts EV charging operators in a difficult position: wholesale electricity prices in the SP15 zone swing sharply intraday — driven by solar generation's "duck curve" — while competitive pressure from 50+ neighboring stations constrains pricing flexibility. Acting on intuition alone is expensive.

This project, developed within Purdue University's VIP-ORSOL initiative, builds an integrated analytical framework to address both sides of this problem. A leakage-controlled LSTM pipeline forecasts real-time SP15 electricity prices 24 hours ahead using market, weather, and fuel data, achieving a test MAE of ~$10.99/MWh and R² of ~0.48 across 5,500+ out-of-sample observations. A complementary Nash equilibrium optimization model then simulates how 51 competing stations price their services, revealing that competition — not capacity pricing — keeps the corridor affordable, and that physical infrastructure expansion matters more than price adjustments for reducing congestion.

Submitted to the SAS Global Forum Curiosity Cup 2026, the forecasting component was awarded Finalist recognition — validating both the technical rigor and the practical framing of the problem.

03 / Module 1

Electricity Price Forecasting

★ SAS Global Forum Curiosity Cup 2026 — Finalist
Formal Problem

Given a multivariate history of the prior 24 hours, predict LMP_RTM_SP15 at hour t + 24. The 24-hour horizon is operationally meaningful: charging operators need advance guidance to plan next-day pricing schedules and reduce exposure to predictable high-cost periods.

⚠️ Leakage Prevention — A Critical Design Constraint

Electricity datasets are uniquely leakage-prone. Day-ahead SP15 prices are highly correlated with real-time prices and were explicitly excluded — a model trained on them would be unrealistically optimistic. Additional safeguards enforced throughout:

  • Strict chronological 60/20/20 split — no shuffling
  • Scaling parameters fit on training data only, applied unchanged to validation and test
  • Lag features and rolling calculations verified to reference only past values
  • Sequence windows checked to confirm no boundary crossings between splits
  • Correlation-based screening removed day-ahead prices and other market leakage pathways

ACF/PACF analysis confirmed a strong 24-hour cycle. Results show clear short-term persistence — dependence on the prior hour and prior day — and stable baseline behavior across years. Residual diagnostics confirm that extreme price spikes remain uncaptured across all linear specifications, motivating the LSTM component.

The ARMA benchmark serves a diagnostic purpose: by establishing what linear persistence alone can explain, it isolates how much the LSTM's nonlinear cross-variable modeling actually contributes — and where both approaches hit the same wall (abrupt evening volatility).

04 / Module 2

Market Equilibrium Optimization

Formal Problem

Model strategic pricing interaction among 51 EV charging stations on the I-210 corridor, where each station's optimal price depends on its competitors. Compute Nash equilibrium prices via iterative best-response. Identify whether pricing power or physical capacity is the binding constraint on corridor performance.

Mathematical Framework

Driver Utility (Eq. 1)
Ui,s = −θeips − βVOTdi,s
Driver evaluates each station on price paid and detour distance. Outside option: home charging at $0.18/kWh.
Logit Choice (Eq. 2)
Pi,s = exp(Ui,s) / Σr∈S∪{0} exp(Ui,r)
Standard multinomial logit. Calibrated to published price elasticity values (as low as −1.5 in fast-charging contexts).
Expected Demand (Eq. 3)
Es(p) = ΣNi=1 ei · Pi,s(p)
Aggregates choice probabilities across all N drivers in the corridor traffic stream.
Profit Maximization (Eq. 5)
maxps (ps − ct) ΣeiPi,s(p) + γ(ΣPi,s·μ − uNs)
Smooth capacity penalty (γ) discourages prices that lead to overload. Solved via iterative best-response with damping factor until convergence.

Equilibrium Findings

01
Prices Stay Stable Under Peak Demand

Despite demand peaking sharply at hour 18 (evening commute surge), equilibrium prices remain stable throughout the day. Competition prevents any station from exploiting peak demand — stations cannot raise prices without losing customers to nearby alternatives, keeping the corridor affordable for drivers.

02
Physical Capacity Is the Binding Constraint

Stations operate below capacity during hours 0–6, but utilization rises sharply from hour 6 onward. Many afternoon and evening stations exceed their rated capacity by 200–300%, confirming that physical infrastructure — not pricing power — is the dominant performance driver. Expanding capacity is substantially more effective than adjusting prices for reducing congestion.

03
Profits Are Evenly Distributed

Profit shares across the 51-station network are spread evenly throughout the day — no station captures a dominant share. During high-demand hours, profit concentration decreases slightly as competition intensifies. The corridor behaves like a near-perfectly-competitive market with limited pricing power.

04
Algorithm Converges Stably

The iterative best-response algorithm shows mild oscillation in early iterations followed by rapid stabilization — confirming the equilibrium is both mathematically stable and computationally tractable. Convergence threshold: 0.01 $/kWh max change per iteration.

The Integrated Picture

Taken together, the two modules tell a coherent story: real-time electricity prices have predictable intraday structure that operators can leverage for load-shifting decisions (forecasting), but competitive pressure means the savings from smart timing cannot easily be converted into pricing power over customers (optimization). The binding constraint is physical capacity, not information asymmetry.

06 / Reflections

What I Learned

🔒

Leakage in Electricity Markets Is Subtle

Day-ahead prices are so highly correlated with real-time prices that including them produces unrealistically optimistic metrics. Building automated verification checks into the pipeline — not just applying a split — is the only reliable safeguard. The discipline of leakage prevention should be architectured into the data pipeline, not reviewed after the fact.

⚡

The Hardest Hours Are a Feature, Not a Bug

The 17–21h ramp window — where both LSTM and ARMA struggled — is not a modeling failure. It reflects a genuine information limit: abrupt grid stress events during the duck curve ramp are driven by conditions not well-represented in the available feature set. Acknowledging this honestly in the paper strengthened the analysis rather than undermining it.

🎯

Classical Models as Diagnostic Tools

The ARMA benchmark served a purpose beyond performance comparison — its residual diagnostics isolated exactly which price dynamics are linear (persistence, 24-hour cycle) and which are not (spikes, ramp events). Without this baseline, the LSTM's contribution would be ambiguous. Building transparent baselines first is a professional habit worth maintaining.

🎲

Game Theory Changes What Questions You Ask

Framing the corridor as a Nash equilibrium problem forced a different set of questions: not just "what is the optimal price?" but "what is the equilibrium price given that all competitors are also optimizing?" This distinction matters — and the finding that competition naturally limits price spikes only emerges from the equilibrium framing, not from optimizing a single station in isolation.

📊

Physical Constraints Beat Pricing Theory

The optimization result that 200–300% overutilization is the dominant problem — not pricing power — was counterintuitive but emerged directly from the data and model. Infrastructure decisions are often framed as pricing problems. This analysis provides a quantitative argument for why capacity investment should precede pricing policy in corridor planning.

🔗

Multi-Source Data Integration Is Underrated

Aligning CAISO prices, PeMS traffic, OpenChargeMap metadata, DESL session records, and weather data to a common hourly index required careful unit conversion, timestamp normalization, and deduplication — and this work was invisible in the final results. The discipline of building a clean, validated, merged dataset is often where the real analytical work lives.
