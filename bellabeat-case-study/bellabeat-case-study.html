01 — Overview

The Business Question

Bellabeat is a women's health-technology company whose Leaf tracker, Time watch, and Spring water bottle all connect to a unified wellness app. The company's leadership wanted to know: what do fitness tracker usage patterns tell us about consumer behavior, and how should those insights shape our marketing strategy?

Using publicly available Fitbit tracking data from Kaggle as a proxy for smart device behavior, this analysis follows the structured Ask → Prepare → Process → Analyze → Share → Act framework from the Google Data Analytics Professional Certificate. The goal is not just to describe the data — it's to produce specific, actionable recommendations for the Leaf product team and marketing function.

Tools used span three environments: BigQuery SQL for initial exploration and quality checking; R (tidyverse, ggplot2, lubridate, janitor) for cleaning, transformation, and statistical visualization; and Tableau for the final analytical charts used to communicate findings to stakeholders.

02 — Prepare & Process

Data Preparation

Issue 1
Mixed 12h/24h Timestamps

The heart rate dataset mixed 12-hour and 24-hour time formats within the same column — row 48 used 24h format while row 4852 switched to "AM" notation. R's separate() was used to split datetime columns into standardized date, time, and AM/PM fields across all affected tables before re-exporting to SQL.

Fix: tidyr::separate() → standardized CSV → re-import to BigQuery
Issue 2
Narrow vs. Wide Format Date Discrepancy

The narrow and wide versions of the minute calories table started on different dates — narrow data began April 12, while wide data began April 13. A SQL ORDER BY Date query confirmed this. Narrow format was retained as it provides more complete coverage.

Fix: SQL SELECT * ORDER BY Date comparison → narrow format selected
Issue 3
Incomplete Coverage in Several Tables

A RIGHT JOIN between the heart rate table and daily steps table revealed that 26 of 33 users had no heart rate data (shown as NULL in the join output). Similar coverage gaps in weight log (8 users) and sleep table (24 users). Only tables with all 33 users retained.

Fix: RIGHT JOIN coverage check → tables with <33 users excluded
Issue 4
ID Consistency Across Tables

A FULL OUTER JOIN between daily steps and daily intensities confirmed all 33 user IDs were identical across the retained tables — no phantom IDs or mismatches.

Fix: FULL OUTER JOIN on ID fields → confirmed consistent across retained tables
Final Data Scope Decision

Minute-level data discarded in favor of daily and hourly data — providing sufficient granularity to identify behavioral patterns without the noise of per-minute variation. Daily calories and daily intensities tables discarded as their content is redundant with the combined daily activity table. Hourly calories, intensities, and steps tables merged on (Id, ActivityHour) after confirming all three contained exactly 22,099 observations.

Finding 1
Intensity Predicts Calorie Burn Better Than Steps

04 — Act

Marketing & Product Recommendations

Four specific, data-grounded recommendations for the Bellabeat Leaf product and app, derived directly from the behavioral patterns identified in the analysis.

01
💡

Promote Intensity Over Step Count

Since intensity (R²=0.80) predicts calorie burn substantially better than steps (R²=0.66), the Leaf product should shift its messaging from step-counting to intensity-based goals. The Bellabeat app could send real-time notifications alerting users when their activity level has been low-intensity for an extended period, prompting them to increase effort for better caloric outcomes.

Source: Intensity vs Calories R² analysis (Finding 1)
02
🎯

Celebrate the 3% Weekly Activity Goal

Users average 2.85% of their week at moderate-or-higher intensity — just below the AHA's 300-minutes/week (~3%) recommendation. The app could track this metric explicitly, sending congratulatory messages when users reach 2.85% (acknowledging they've matched the average user) and again at 3% (confirming they've met AHA guidelines). Two achievable milestones create a progression incentive.

Source: Activity time breakdown analysis (Finding 2)
03
📅

Target Low-Activity Days with Motivation

Sunday and Thursday show consistently lower activity levels and lower heavy-activity distances. The Leaf product could send targeted reminders on these days encouraging users to increase both workout duration and intensity. The marketing team could design campaigns emphasizing consistency — framing Sunday and Thursday workouts as bridging sessions between the natural peaks of Tuesday and Saturday.

Source: Weekly distance trends by activity level (Finding 3)
04
⏰

Use the 2 PM Dip as a Notification Window

The noticeable drop in activity intensity between 2 PM and 3 PM creates a precise opportunity for motivational prompts from the Leaf device. A brief vibration or app notification at 2 PM encouraging users to stay active — timed to the behavioral pattern in the data — is more likely to be effective than generic reminders. The 5–7 PM peak window could also receive intensity-specific coaching to maximize calorie burn during the day's most active period.

Source: Hourly variation in intensity and calorie burn (Finding 4)
Limitations & Future Directions
⚠️
Small sample: 33 users from 2016 may not represent Bellabeat's target demographic or modern usage patterns. Larger, more recent datasets would improve generalizability.
💤
Sleep data excluded: The sleep table had only 24 users. Including it would have allowed analysis of sleep-activity relationships — a key Bellabeat product dimension. Future studies should prioritize sleep data collection completeness.
💧
Spring & Time data gaps: Hydration (Spring) and stress (Time) data would round out the wellness picture. Cross-product analysis could uncover whether users who hydrate more are also more active.
05 — Reflections

What I Learned

🔍

Data Quality Discovery is Iterative

The timestamp inconsistency in the heart rate table (row 48 vs row 4852 using different formats) was only found by inspecting specific row indices — not by running a general NULL or type check. Quality issues hide in format inconsistencies, not just missing values. SQL and R together make a strong combination: SQL for coverage counts and date range validation, R for row-level inspection and transformation.

📐

R² as a Comparative Tool, Not Just a Goodness-of-Fit Metric

Running the same regression structure on two different predictors and comparing R² values (0.80 vs 0.66) is a simple but powerful analytical technique for stakeholder communication. It transforms an abstract statistical concept into a clear business-relevant answer: "which variable should Bellabeat's app prioritize?" The ggpmisc::stat_poly_eq(use_label("R2")) pattern makes this straightforward to produce in ggplot2.

🗂️

Structured Frameworks Prevent Analytical Drift

The Ask-Prepare-Process-Analyze-Share-Act framework kept the analysis focused on the original business question throughout. Without it, it would be easy to get absorbed in interesting data patterns that don't connect back to marketing strategy. The framework acts as a discipline: every visualization and finding should trace back to a stakeholder decision.

🛠️

The Right Tool for Each Job

Using BigQuery for coverage and quality queries, R for cleaning and statistical charts, and Tableau for final polished visualizations made each stage more efficient than trying to do everything in one environment. BigQuery handles large tables and JOIN-based quality checks well; ggplot2 excels at flexible exploratory charts; Tableau produces presentation-ready interactive graphics with minimal friction.

💬

Translating Data to Decisions Requires Context

Finding that users spend 2.85% of their time at moderate+ intensity would be meaningless without the AHA's 3% benchmark to frame it. External reference points — clinical guidelines, industry standards, competitive norms — are what make raw numbers actionable. Good analysis always asks: "compared to what?"

⚠️

Be Honest About Dataset Limitations

Explicitly excluding the heart rate, weight, and sleep tables — rather than using incomplete data — was the right call, even though it narrowed the analysis. Overstating confidence in conclusions drawn from 7 or 8 users would be misleading. Documenting what was excluded and why is part of responsible analysis, not a weakness.
